## OFFICIAL CHARACTER BIBLE — EXTENDED EDITION

*"Nu sgarro nun se scorda. 'A famiglia nun se tradisce."*
*A violation is never forgotten. Family is never betrayed.*

---

### A note on this document

The original repository establishes seven core character files (`characters/boss.md`, `underboss.md`, `wife.md`,
`enemy.md`, `police.md`, `judge.md`, `priest.md`) as role-based placeholders — enough for a pitch deck, not enough for a
ten-episode writers' room. This Extended Character Bible builds each of those seven roles into a full, dramatizable
person, gives each one a name, a psychology, a voice, and a season-by-season arc — and, per the "Canon vs. Development"
section of the Story Bible, formally proposes three additional Ranieri-generation characters (the boss's children)
required to dramatize the show's central generational theme, plus one additional Esposito-side character required for
the same reason on the antagonist side.

**Everything below is new development built on locked Story Bible canon** — the Ranieri/Esposito family structure, the
Peace, the three-level power structure, the code, and the violence philosophy are all treated as fixed. Names,
psychology, and specific relationships are proposed here for the first time and should be treated as the working canon
going forward unless revised by the creator.

This document is organized in seven parts:

- **Part One**: Cast overview, casting philosophy, and the family tree.
- **Part Two**: The Ranieri family — six characters.
- **Part Three**: Clan Esposito — two characters.
- **Part Four**: The Institutional Fringe — three characters.
- **Part Five**: Voice & dialogue guide, per character.
- **Part Six**: Relationship matrix.
- **Part Seven**: Season-by-season arc tracking for all eleven characters.

## PART ONE — CAST OVERVIEW

---

### 1. Casting Philosophy & Ensemble Shape

VESUVIO's eleven-character core ensemble (expanded from the original seven-role skeleton) is built around a deliberate
structural symmetry: **six Ranieri, two Esposito, three Institutional Fringe.** This is not accidental. The show's
central conflict is a family fracturing from within more than it is a war between two equal armies — Esposito is written
lean and sharp precisely because it should feel like a smaller, hungrier, more dangerous force pressing on a larger,
more established one. The Institutional Fringe's three characters exist outside both camps by design, so the audience
always has somewhere to stand that is not simply "Team Ranieri."

**Casting mandate (binding, per the authenticity standard set in Story Bible §23):** every role should be cast with
actors capable of credible Neapolitan dialect performance, coached to the same standard as the show's original
soundtrack. No role should be written or cast as a "type" — every character below is built with a specific, particular
psychology precisely so that no actor is asked to play "the mafia wife" or "the corrupt cop" as a genre archetype.

**Ensemble weight (approximate, for scripting and episode-count planning purposes — to be finalized against the Season
Bible):**

| Tier | Characters | Season 1 Episode Presence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lead | Domenico Ranieri, Assunta Ranieri, Michele Ranieri | All 10 episodes |
| Co-Lead | Rocco Fusco, Bianca Ranieri, Nunzio Esposito | 8–10 episodes |
| Major Supporting | Gennaro Ranieri, Antonio Sarno, Fabiana Esposito | 6–9 episodes |
| Supporting | Renata Colombo, Don Peppino Iervolino | 4–7 episodes |

## PART TWO — FAMIGLIA RANIERI

---

### 3. Domenico Ranieri — Il Capo ("The Boss")

**Full name:** Domenico Antonio Ranieri. **Called:** "Mimì" by his wife and oldest friends only — never by subordinates.
**Age:** mid-50s. **Position:** Capo of Famiglia Ranieri; architect and principal guarantor of the Peace.

**One-line essence:** A man who ended a war and has spent ten years terrified of being the one who starts the next.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to die having handed his children a Naples that does not require them to become what he became.
*Need:* to admit, to someone, that the Peace was never a solution — only a postponement — before that admission is
forced out of him by events he can no longer control.
*Fear:* that his caution, which he has always called wisdom, is actually just fear wearing his father's clothes.
*Wound:* he was present, as a young man, for the single worst act of the founding-generation war (specific event TBD in
Season Bible/flashback planning) — an act he has never confessed, literally or figuratively, to anyone, including Don
Peppino.

**Backstory.** Domenico came of age inside the war, not after it — he was in his late teens and early twenties during
the worst of the founding-generation conflict (Story Bible §6), old enough to have blood on his hands and young enough
that it was his father's war, not yet his own. When his father was killed, Domenico inherited leadership of a shattered,
exhausted organization at an age most men are still finding their footing in the world. The Peace he negotiated in his
forties was not an act of statesmanship so much as an act of sheer exhaustion shared by every surviving Capo of his
generation — but he has spent the ten years since building a personal mythology around it that lets him sleep at night.

**Present-day dynamic.** Domenico governs by delay. His signature move, dramatically, is the postponed decision — he is
a boss who has learned that most problems, given enough silence and enough time, solve themselves or reveal their real
shape. This has served him for a decade. It will not serve him in Season 1, where the inciting betrayal (Story Bible §6)
is precisely the kind of problem that punishes delay. Watching Domenico's oldest survival skill become his greatest
liability is the season's central character engine on the Ranieri side.

**Relationship to the code (Story Bible §11):** Domenico believes in the code more sincerely than almost anyone around
him — not as strategy, but as the only thing that gave meaning to what the war cost him. This sincerity is both his
dignity and his blind spot.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 forces Domenico to choose, repeatedly, between the caution that built the Peace and
the decisiveness that might be the only thing capable of saving it — and the season should end with him having made the
harder choice one beat too late. Season 2 is his war. Season 3 is his reckoning (Story Bible §25) and, per the locked
series arc, his story's conclusion.

### 4. Assunta Ranieri — ("The Wife")

**Full name:** Assunta Ranieri, née Coppola. **Age:** early 50s. **Position:** Domenico's wife of nearly thirty years;
the most underestimated power center in the series.

**One-line essence:** The person who has been running the family's real business — its relationships, its memory, its
loyalty accounting — for three decades, while everyone around her, including her husband, calls it "keeping the house."

**Development note:** the original Story Bible flags this role as a priority expansion area (§9, §24), citing real-world
documentation of women's significant — and often decisive — informal and formal leadership roles within comparable
organizations, particularly when male leadership is compromised. This Character Bible resolves that flag: **Assunta is
written as a co-architect of the Peace, not a bystander to it.** She does not hold the title of Capo and does not want
it — but no major decision in the Ranieri household of the last twenty years has been made without her, even when the
men in the room believed otherwise.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* for at least one of her children to survive this world without being consumed by it — and she has a private,
unspoken ranking of which child that might still be possible for.
*Need:* to be seen, once, by Domenico, as the reason the Peace held, not merely as its beneficiary.
*Fear:* that she has spent thirty years managing a machine that is about to grind up her own children the way it ground
up her husband.
*Wound:* she has kept exactly one secret from Domenico in thirty years of marriage — the nature of that secret is a
Season 1–2 engine, deliberately not specified further at this canon stage (see Appendix C, Open Development Questions).

**Present-day dynamic.** Assunta's power operates entirely through indirection: the seating arrangement at a dinner, the
wife of a rival she chooses to embrace or ignore at a funeral, the debt she calls in on someone else's behalf without
ever raising her voice. She is the character best positioned to *see* the shape of the coming crisis before Domenico
does, precisely because she has always had to read the room he assumes he controls.

**Relationship to the code:** Assunta understands the code as a language of relationships rather than a set of rules —
she is fluent in it in a way Domenico, who thinks of it as principle, is not. This distinction should be visible in how
each of them handles the same crisis.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 tracks Assunta's growing recognition that discretion — her lifelong tool — is no
longer sufficient to protect her children, forcing her toward more direct action by the finale. Her arc across the
series is the show's clearest embodiment of the "shelter and cage" theme (Story Bible §20): the same skill set that has
protected this family for decades becomes, under new pressure, dangerously insufficient.

### 5. Rocco Fusco — Il Sottocapo ("The Underboss")

**Full name:** Rocco Fusco. **Age:** mid-50s, Domenico's exact contemporary. **Position:** Sottocapo; Domenico's oldest
friend, raised in the same Rione stairwell.

**One-line essence:** The man who has spent thirty years being the fist so Domenico could be the face — and who has
never once been asked what he wanted instead.

**Development note, resolving Story Bible §9's flagged ambiguity ("the audience should never be fully certain the
Sottocapo's loyalty is uncomplicated"):** Rocco's loyalty to Domenico is genuine and lifelong — but genuine loyalty and
total safety are not the same thing in this show's moral universe. Rocco is written not as a closet traitor but as a man
whose loyalty has a floor: there is a line past which he will not follow Domenico, and neither Rocco nor the audience
should know exactly where that line is until Season 1 forces the question.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to be the reason Domenico survives what's coming, in a way visible enough that it is finally acknowledged.
*Need:* to stop mistaking usefulness for belonging — Rocco has spent his whole life being indispensable to the Ranieri
family without ever quite being *of* it, not by blood.
*Fear:* that when the moment comes to choose between Domenico's orders and Domenico's actual wellbeing, he will make the
wrong call and lose the only family he has ever had, either way.
*Wound:* he has a son (not previously canonized — proposed here as a Season 2–3 development thread, see Appendix C) who
wants nothing to do with this world, and Rocco does not know whether to be proud or heartbroken about it, and privately
suspects both feelings are the same feeling.

**Present-day dynamic.** Rocco is Domenico's blunt instrument and his conscience in equal measure — the only person in
the Ranieri orbit who can tell Domenico he is wrong to his face and survive it, because of thirty years of shared
history no title can replace or threaten. This makes him the show's most efficient device for externalizing Domenico's
internal conflicts into actual dialogue scenes.

**Relationship to the code:** Rocco lives the code more literally and more painfully than anyone else in the ensemble —
silence, no tears, debts paid — because he has never had Domenico's authority to occasionally bend it.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 tests Rocco's floor directly, via an order from Domenico that Rocco privately
believes is a mistake. How Rocco resolves that tension (comply, quietly redirect, or refuse) is a top-priority Season
Bible decision, flagged in Appendix C.

### 6. Michele Ranieri — The Heir Apparent

**Full name:** Michele Ranieri. **Age:** ~30. **Position:** Domenico and Assunta's eldest child; the son groomed, from
birth, to inherit.

**One-line essence:** A man who has never once asked himself if he wants this life, because no one ever told him wanting
was an option.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to prove himself worthy of a throne he has never questioned wanting.
*Need:* to have one real conversation with his father that isn't, in some way, a test.
*Fear:* that when the real crisis comes, he will freeze — and that everyone, especially his father, already suspects
this about him.
*Wound:* he watched his father hesitate at a formative moment in his adolescence (specific event TBD) and has spent his
adult life overcorrecting into decisiveness that sometimes reads as recklessness.

**Present-day dynamic.** Michele represents "tradition" in the show's central tradition-vs-modernity theme (Story Bible
§20) — but written specifically to avoid being merely his father's echo. His loyalty to the old code is not naive; it is
a considered, adult choice made by someone who has seen the alternative (his siblings' paths) and finds it unbearable,
not merely unfamiliar. His central Season 1 relationship is with Gennaro — an older brother watching a younger one drift
toward exactly the mistake that could destroy everything Michele has spent his life preparing to inherit.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 is Michele's first real test of command — likely a delegated operational
responsibility from Domenico that goes wrong in a way that is not entirely Michele's fault, but that he will carry as if
it were. Season 2–3 tracks his full step into leadership, complicated by everything Season 1 costs him.

### 7. Bianca Ranieri — The One Who Wants Out

**Full name:** Bianca Ranieri. **Age:** ~27. **Position:** Domenico and Assunta's second child; a trainee physician (or
lawyer — flagged, Appendix C, for Season Bible to fix) building a life in "Napoli di Sopra" that she has spent a decade
trying to make real.

**One-line essence:** The daughter who left, except she never actually left — she just moved the war inside herself
instead.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* a life where her last name is a fact about her family, not a fact about her.
*Need:* to stop believing that distance is the same thing as freedom.
*Fear:* that she is exactly as capable of the family's ruthlessness as Michele is, and has simply never been tested — a
fear Season 1 is built to confirm or disprove.
*Wound:* an early-adulthood event (specific TBD) that first made her choose the "legitimate" path — proposed as
connected to Story Bible §14's violence philosophy, i.e., she witnessed a specific consequence of the family business up
close, young, and it never left her.

**Present-day dynamic.** Bianca is the show's primary lens into Napoli di Sopra (Story Bible §4) — her working life
(hospital or courtroom) gives the show a legitimate reason to cross the membrane between the two Naples every episode
without contrivance. Her relationship with Assunta is the show's richest mother-daughter dynamic: Assunta is quietly,
fiercely proud of the distance Bianca has achieved and simultaneously the person best positioned to see that Bianca has
never actually achieved it.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 should force Bianca back across the membrane at least once, professionally or
personally, in a way that costs her something in her "legitimate" life — the show's clearest single dramatization of the
idea that there is no clean exit from this family, only different distances.

### 8. Gennaro Ranieri — The Wildcard

**Full name:** Gennaro Ranieri. **Age:** ~23. **Position:** Domenico and Assunta's youngest child; not yet formally
inside the business, orbiting its edges.

**One-line essence:** The son nobody has decided what to do with yet — including himself.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to matter to his father the way Michele matters, without doing what Michele does.
*Need:* someone to take him seriously before he does something to force the issue.
*Fear:* that he is the family's disappointment by default, simply for being the one without an assigned role.
*Wound:* growing up as "the baby" in a house run on hierarchy and usefulness, with no clear function of his own.

**Present-day dynamic.** Gennaro is the character most vulnerable to Esposito's orbit (Story Bible §16) — not through
ideological conversion, but through the much more dangerous mechanism of simply being flattered and taken seriously by
someone outside his family for the first time. This is the show's most direct dramatization of "gradual entry" as
described in Story Bible §10: Gennaro should not have a single dramatic "turn," but a series of small, individually
forgivable steps that add up to real danger by mid-season.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 tracks Gennaro's drift toward an unsanctioned relationship with someone in or near
Clan Esposito's orbit — the season's most dangerous slow-burn thread precisely because every individual step looks
minor. This is a top-priority thread for Season Bible / Beat Sheet development given its direct connection to the
inciting-betrayal ambiguity established in Story Bible §6.

### 9. Nunzio Esposito — ("The Enemy")

**Full name:** Nunzio Esposito. **Age:** early 30s. **Position:** De facto operational head of Clan Esposito's younger
faction — not yet, and possibly never, formally "Capo" in the traditional sense his father's generation would recognize.

**One-line essence:** A man who has already won the argument about what Naples' future looks like and is still waiting
for anyone with real power to admit it.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* formal, undeniable recognition — from Ranieri, from the old guard, from the city itself — that his generation's
Naples has already arrived.
*Need:* to prove, mostly to himself, that his ambition is not simply his father's failure worn as a mask.
*Fear:* that he is building something that will collapse the moment he is no longer young and hungry enough to hold it
together by force of personality alone — that he has no Peace of his own to hand down, only momentum.
*Wound:* the specific mechanism by which his father's/elder generation's leadership was displaced (Story Bible §24, left
open) is proposed here to be *personal* to Nunzio — he was present for, and possibly forced into, whatever that
displacement was, and it is the thing he has never told even Fabiana the full truth about.

**Crucial writers'-room note (per Story Bible §16):** Nunzio is not written as a villain. His core grievance — that the
Peace has always favored Ranieri's entrenched position, negotiated by men who no longer represent the street's present
reality — is meant to land as *legitimate*, even when his methods do not. Any scene that makes Nunzio simply cruel
without cause should be flagged in table reads; his aggression should always be traceable to a specific, comprehensible
stake.

**Present-day dynamic.** Nunzio's central relationship is with Fabiana — he provides the face, the charisma, and the
willingness to act; she provides the strategic patience he does not naturally have. Their relationship should be written
as a genuine partnership with real friction, not a puppet-and-puppeteer dynamic.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 tracks Nunzio's calculated escalation against the Peace — testing its limits before
committing to breaking it outright — culminating in the season's point-of-no-return finale (Story Bible §25). Season 2
is his open war; his Season 3 fate is a top-priority open question (Appendix C) directly tied to how the series
ultimately wants to resolve its tradition-vs-modernity theme.

### 10. Fabiana Esposito — The Strategist

**Full name:** Fabiana Esposito. **Age:** ~36, Nunzio's elder sister. **Position:** Proposed new character (not in the
original seven-role roster) — the unofficial architect of Clan Esposito's rise, deliberately kept out of public view.

**Development rationale:** Story Bible §16 requires Esposito's internal structure to mirror Ranieri's generational
tension "inverted," and requires the faction to feel dangerous through more than just youth and aggression. A single
antagonist character cannot carry both the show's need for a charismatic, legitimate-grievance rival (Nunzio) and the
strategic sophistication necessary to actually threaten the Ranieri family's decades of institutional entrenchment
(Story Bible §8, Level 3). Fabiana resolves this structurally, and gives the Esposito side its own version of the
Domenico/Assunta dynamic — a visible leader and an invisible architect — deepening the show's thematic symmetry between
the two families.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* real institutional power (Story Bible §8, Level 3) for Clan Esposito — not just street dominance, which she
considers a means, never an end.
*Need:* to be right about Nunzio — she has staked everything, including her own safety, on her belief that her brother
is capable of holding what she is building for him.
*Fear:* that she is smarter than the role available to her in this world allows her to be — and that the only way to
fully use that intelligence is to eventually stop operating in her brother's shadow.
*Wound:* proposed connection to the displaced-elder-leadership mechanism (Story Bible §24) — Fabiana may have been the
one who orchestrated it, a possibility to be resolved in Season Bible development and treated, until then, as the show's
darkest open question about its own antagonist family.

**Season arc, in brief:** Fabiana operates almost entirely through intermediaries and institutional-level moves (Level
3) in Season 1, rarely appearing in direct confrontation scenes — reserving her direct emergence into visibility as a
mid-to-late-season escalation device.

### 11. Antonio Sarno — ("The Police")

**Full name:** Antonio Sarno. **Age:** late 40s. **Position:** Vice Questore, Polizia di Stato, Naples. Born and raised
in the Rione — the Ranieri family's home turf.

**One-line essence:** A man enforcing the law in the only neighborhood in Naples where he will always, first, be
recognized as somebody's son.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to believe that the badge he earned by leaving the Rione's usual paths actually changed something, rather than
simply changing his address.
*Need:* one case, one moment, where doing his job and honoring where he comes from are not in direct opposition.
*Fear:* that he has spent twenty years being useful to both sides without ever being loyal to either — and that this
makes him the least trustworthy person in the entire ensemble, including to himself.
*Wound:* a specific personal connection to the Ranieri family (proposed: he and Domenico were childhood friends before
their paths diverged completely — a mirror to the Domenico/Rocco friendship, showing two different outcomes from the
same stairwell) — see Appendix C for confirmation status.

**Present-day dynamic.** Sarno occupies the "ambiguous middle" role specified in Story Bible §17 — neither fully
complicit nor fully clean. His investigative attention, formally directed at "organized crime" in general, is
constantly, personally complicated by the fact that the family at the center of it raised him alongside its own
children. He is the show's clearest embodiment of "family before law" tested on someone who did not, technically, choose
this family.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 should place Sarno in a position where the inciting betrayal (Story Bible §6) crosses
his desk in some form — official knowledge of an unofficial event — forcing an early, private choice about what he does
with it, whose consequences echo across the whole season.

---

### 12. Renata Colombo — ("The Judge")

**Full name:** Renata Colombo. **Age:** late 50s. **Position:** Giudice, Tribunale di Napoli. Not Neapolitan by birth
(proposed: originally from northern Italy, relocated to Naples early in her career) — an outsider's clarity as a
deliberate character tool.

**One-line essence:** The one person in this world operating entirely by the rules everyone else has learned to treat as
optional — and paying, daily, for the privilege.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* a conviction that actually holds — not a headline, an arrest, or a symbolic gesture, but a case that survives
appeal, survives witness intimidation, survives the system's genuine and constant undertow toward letting things quietly
go.
*Need:* to stop needing this fight to be winnable in order to keep fighting it.
*Fear:* that she has become useful to the state primarily as a symbol of resolve rather than as an actual threat to the
people she is meant to prosecute.
*Wound:* proposed connection to an earlier, career-defining case (TBD) in which a key witness or collaborator was killed
or intimidated into silence — the origin of both her resolve and her isolation.

**Present-day dynamic.** Colombo is the show's outside-in perspective on Naples — she sees the city's contradictions
(Story Bible §3–4) with an outsider's eye that the born-and-raised characters cannot access, and this makes her scenes a
valuable tonal contrast tool: cold, procedural, fluorescent-lit, per Story Bible §18's Courthouse location notes.

**Season arc, in brief:** Colombo's Season 1 thread should track her slowly assembling a case that will not fully mature
until Season 2–3, positioning her as a long-fuse threat to both families rather than a same-season resolution —
consistent with Story Bible §7's guidance that institutional pressure is a background force, not a procedural A-plot.

---

### 13. Don Peppino Iervolino — ("The Priest")

**Full name:** Don Giuseppe "Peppino" Iervolino. **Age:** late 60s. **Position:** Parish priest of the Rione's church
for over thirty years; has baptized, married, and buried three generations of both the Ranieri and Esposito families.

**One-line essence:** The only man in Naples both families still trust enough to be honest with, in a world built on the
assumption that honesty is always, eventually, a weapon.

**Psychology.**
*Want:* to die having kept at least one soul in this parish from the fate he has watched claim so many others.
*Need:* to reconcile thirty years of holding confession for men whose sins he cannot report and will not absolve in any
way that changes their actions.
*Fear:* that his silence — sacred, legally protected, spiritually mandated — has functioned, in practice,
indistinguishably from the code's *'o silenzio* (Story Bible §11), and that he cannot honestly say his motives have
stayed pure across thirty years of proximity to power.
*Wound:* he baptized Domenico's children; he baptized Nunzio and Fabiana; he has buried people from both families whose
deaths he privately suspects were not accidents — and he has never once broken the seal of confession to prevent a death
he saw coming, a fact he has made a kind of peace with that the show should never let the audience find fully
comfortable.

**Present-day dynamic.** Don Peppino is the show's confessional-scene anchor (Story Bible §12) and the character best
positioned to speak plainly to any member of either family without immediate risk — a narrative utility the show should
use sparingly and meaningfully, never as a shortcut to deliver exposition.

**Season arc, in brief:** Season 1 should test Don Peppino directly — a confession or confidence that puts him in
possession of information that could prevent real harm, forcing the season's clearest dramatization of the
code-vs-confessional question (Story Bible §12) onto the one character built to embody it.

### 14. Voice & Dialogue Guide

| Character | Register | Dialect Density | Speech Pattern Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Domenico Ranieri | Formal, measured, deliberately unhurried | High in private, moderated in institutional scenes | Uses silence as punctuation; rarely raises his voice — the one time in a season he does should be a major event |
| Assunta Ranieri | Precise, indirect, socially fluent | High | Speaks in implication; her most important sentences are often about something other than what she's actually addressing |
| Rocco Fusco | Blunt, dry, economical | High | Short sentences; humor as pressure release; the character most likely to say the thing everyone else is avoiding |

### 15. Physicality & Casting Notes

*(High-level notes for casting-breakdown purposes; full detailed physical descriptions to be finalized alongside casting
director input and are intentionally left flexible here to avoid over-constraining casting.)*

- **Domenico:** presence built on stillness, not size — should be castable as a man other characters visibly adjust
  their own behavior around before he's said a word.
- **Assunta:** should never be costumed or shot in a way that reads as decorative — her authority is in bearing, not
  glamour.
- **Rocco:** physically capable, visibly weathered — a man whose body has done the family's harder work for thirty
  years.
- **Michele / Bianca / Gennaro:** deliberately cast to be legible as siblings without being interchangeable — a family
  resemblance in bearing more than in specific features.
- **Nunzio:** magnetic, contemporary, should read as someone who would be compelling on camera even outside this world —
  his charisma is the point.
- **Fabiana:** deliberately unshowy — should be the least "camera-ready" major character by design, someone easy to
  underestimate at first glance.
- **Sarno:** should visually read as capable of belonging in both a Questura briefing room and a Rione kitchen without a
  costume change telegraphing the shift.
- **Colombo:** visually coded as an outsider throughout — the show's one major character for whom "belonging in Naples"
  is never the visual goal.
- **Don Peppino:** should avoid every visual cliché of the "movie priest" — the World Bible / Moodbook should specify a
  grounded, specific parish-priest aesthetic, not gothic austerity.

### 19. Character Contradictions (Writers'-Room Tool)

*A working list of the productive internal contradiction each character should carry into every scene — useful for
keeping dialogue and choices from flattening into "type."*

- **Domenico:** believes in decisiveness, practices delay.
- **Assunta:** the most powerful person in the room, by design never seen as such.
- **Rocco:** the most loyal man in the family, and not family.
- **Michele:** desperate to be seen as ready, terrified he isn't.
- **Bianca:** built a whole life on distance, has never actually left.
- **Gennaro:** wants to matter, doesn't yet understand the cost of mattering in this family.
- **Nunzio:** right about the future, dangerous about the method.
- **Fabiana:** the smartest person in either family, structurally unable to lead openly.
- **Sarno:** enforces a law he was raised to see as optional.
- **Colombo:** most committed to justice, least equipped to see Naples as its residents do.
- **Don Peppino:** holds the one space built on total honesty, and has never once acted on what he's heard there.
