# 1. Logline & Premise

For ten years, an uneasy peace has held the criminal underworld of Naples
together. When a single act of betrayal breaks that peace, three
generations of one family — bound by blood, honor, and the mountain that
watches over them — are forced to choose between loyalty to the past and
survival in the present.

**VESUVIO: Blood of Naples** is a modern mafia drama set in contemporary
Naples, Italy. It is not a nostalgia piece about "the old ways" — it is a
story about what happens when the old codes collide with a modern world
that no longer respects them: surveillance, global finance, fractured
alliances, and a new generation unwilling to inherit their parents' rules
without question.

The city itself is a character. Mount Vesuvius, dormant but never silent,
looms over every frame — a constant reminder that peace here is always
temporary.

# 2. The World of Naples

Naples is presented not as postcard Italy, but as a living, breathing,
contradictory city: sun-bleached palazzos beside crumbling social housing;
Baroque churches beside abandoned factories; tourists on the Lungomare
unaware of the war being fought three streets inland.

The show draws a hard line between two Naples:

- **La Napoli di sopra** ("Upper Naples") — the historic center, the
  tourist economy, the political and legal establishment, the world that
  pretends the Camorra does not exist.
- **La Napoli di sotto** ("Lower Naples") — the *rioni* (housing-block
  neighborhoods), the ports, the outer *quartieri* — where the Camorra is
  not a rumor but a government.

The series lives almost entirely in the second Naples, occasionally
crossing into the first to show how thin the line between them really is.

# 3. Timeline & History

- **1980s–90s:** The founding generation consolidates territory in the
  chaos following Naples' historical clan wars. The family at the center
  of the show is established during this period.
- **10 years before the pilot:** A fragile truce ("*'a Pace*" — "the
  Peace") is struck between the major clans after a war that nearly
  destroyed all of them. This truce is the bedrock the entire present-day
  story stands on.
- **Present day (Season 1):** The Peace begins to crack. An act of
  betrayal — deliberately left ambiguous in early episodes — triggers the
  central conflict of the series.
- **Series-long arc:** Multi-season structure tracking the slow collapse
  and violent reformation of power in Naples, mirrored against Vesuvius
  itself — dormant, then erupting.

# 4. Politics & Power Structure

Power in the world of VESUVIO operates on three interlocking levels:

1. **The Street** — territorial control of neighborhoods, extortion
   (*'o pizzo*), drug distribution, and local enforcement.
2. **The Business** — legitimate-front companies (construction, waste
   management, import/export, real estate) used to launder and expand
   influence into the legal economy.
3. **The Institutions** — a shifting, deniable network of relationships
   with police, local politicians, and the judiciary. Corruption here is
   never absolute; it is negotiated, situational, and always at risk of
   exposure — a core source of dramatic tension.

The series is explicit that the Camorra is not a single hierarchical
"mafia" like fictional depictions of Cosa Nostra — it is a decentralized
network of semi-independent *clans*, each with its own boss, territory,
and rules, sometimes allied, sometimes at war, held together only by
temporary agreements like "the Peace."

---

# 5. Camorra Structure

A simplified in-world hierarchy used consistently across the series:

- **Il Capo (The Boss)** — head of a clan; final authority on territory,
  war, and peace.
- **Il Sottocapo (The Underboss)** — second-in-command; often the boss's
  most trusted enforcer or a designated successor.
- **I Capizona (Zone Captains)** — control individual neighborhoods or
  business sectors on the boss's behalf.
- **Gli Affiliati (Affiliates / Soldiers)** — street-level enforcers,
  runners, and lookouts; the entry point into the organization.
- **I Prestanome ("Name-lenders")** — legitimate businesspeople who front
  companies for the clan, often unwillingly.
- **Gli Amici degli Amici ("Friends of Friends")** — the deniable outer
  ring: officials, lawyers, and intermediaries who benefit from the clan
  without formally belonging to it.

# 6. The Families

*(Full biographical detail for individual characters lives in
`/characters/`. This section covers the family/clan units.)*

- **Famiglia Ranieri** — the show's central family; controllers of the
  docks and construction trade; architects and guardians of "the Peace."
- **Clan Esposito** — a rising, younger, more volatile faction impatient
  with the old rules; the primary antagonistic force in Season 1.
- **The Institutional Fringe** — a recurring set of police, legal, and
  political figures who orbit both families without full allegiance to
  either.

# 8. Rules & Codes of Conduct

The world of VESUVIO operates on an unwritten but rigidly enforced code,
echoed directly in the show's theme song:

- **Silence (*'o silenzio*)** — nothing is said that doesn't need to be
  said; information is currency, and leaking it is a capital offense.
- **Respect (*'o rispetto*)** — earned, never given; publicly enforced,
  privately negotiated.
- **No mistakes, no tears (*nu sgarro, nu chianto*)** — emotional
  display, especially in front of rivals, is treated as weakness.
- **Debts are always paid (*'a morte se sconta*)** — literally and
  figuratively; the show treats debt (financial, moral, blood) as its
  central engine of plot.
- **Family before law** — but never stated as ideology; shown, episode
  after episode, as the operating assumption of every character.

---

# 9. Religion & Ritual

Catholic ritual is woven through the show not as decoration but as a
genuine, contradictory moral framework the characters live inside:
baptisms, funerals, and confession scenes double as plot delivery
mechanisms and character studies. The **priest** (see `/characters/`) is
a recurring figure who represents the story's central moral question:
can the code and the confessional coexist?

---

# 10. Money & Economy

The economic backbone of the family's power runs through:

- Port logistics and shipping contracts (legitimate front)
- Construction and public-works contracts (legitimate front)
- Territorial extortion, *'o pizzo* (illegitimate)
- Narcotics distribution (illegitimate)
- Real estate laundering (grey zone)

The show is careful to depict money as a *structural* force rather than a
prop — every major plot turn in Season 1 can be traced back to a specific
financial pressure point.

---

# 11. Weapons & Violence

Violence in VESUVIO is treated as consequence, not spectacle. Guidelines
for the writers' room and director:

- Violence is sudden, brief, and costly — never choreographed for
  entertainment.
- The show avoids fetishizing weaponry; guns and blades are shown as
  tools of last resort, not identity markers.
- Aftermath (grief, cover-up, institutional consequence) is given equal
  or greater screen time than the act itself.

# 13. Themes & Tone

- **Loyalty vs. survival** — what happens when doing right by the family
  means doing wrong by yourself.
- **Tradition vs. modernity** — a younger generation inheriting a code
  built for a world that no longer exists.
- **The city as inheritance** — Naples itself as something fought over,
  loved, and feared in equal measure.

Tonally, the reference point is prestige crime drama — grounded,
character-first, morally complex — rather than genre pastiche.

---

# 14. Series Arc

**Season 1** establishes the Peace, the families, and the betrayal that
breaks it — ending on a point of no return.
**Season 2** tracks open conflict between the families as institutional
pressure closes in.
**Season 3** resolves the founding generation's story and hands the world
to its successors, closing the thematic loop the theme song opens: *"Ccà
cumandammo nuje"* — "We command here."
